AASHTO — American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials
The primary engineering standards body for highway design in the United States. The 1993 AASHTO Guide for Design of Pavement Structures is the foundational reference for all structural number calculations in this estimator.
1993 Guide for Design of Pavement Structures, Table 3-1
Required structural number (SN) by traffic loading category and design life. Used to determine how much total structural capacity the pavement system must provide. All six traffic categories in the estimator map directly to AASHTO ESAL ranges and their corresponding SN values.
Six traffic categories: Very Light (SN 1.5) through Heavy (SN 4.1) at 20yr design life
Sourced
1993 Guide, Table 2-4
Base depth recommendations by subgrade soil type (AASHTO M145 classification) and traffic loading. All 13 soil groups, all six traffic categories. Pre-fills the base depth input when soil type and traffic are selected.
13 soil groups × 6 traffic categories = 78 base depth values
vlight, medlow, and medhigh columns are interpolated between the published light, medium, and heavy values.
Sourced
1993 Guide, Table 2-5 — Layer coefficients for base materials
Structural contribution per inch of base material. Used to calculate how much of the required SN the base layer provides, leaving the remainder to be supplied by asphalt thickness. Two coefficients are used:
Conventional aggregate (CBR 80–100%): a2 interpolated 0.14 – 0.18
In-Place BaseGrade (CBR 125%): a2 = 0.20 (treated base category)
Important: AASHTO Table 2-5 does not specifically reference liquid cross-linker treatment. The a2 = 0.20 assignment is the closest published category for a base material exceeding the standard crushed stone threshold. This assignment is defensible but may be challenged in formal engineering review.

National Academies of Sciences — Transportation Research Board
Independent research arm of the National Academies conducting peer-reviewed transportation research. The NCHRP Report cited here is the primary source for the foundation failure attribution figure.
NCHRP Report 602
Documented proportion of road failures attributable to foundation layer failure versus surface or structural failure. This figure is the single most important driver of lifecycle maintenance savings — it determines what portion of ongoing maintenance spend In-Place BaseGrade eliminates by treating the root cause.
Foundation failure attribution: 78% of road failures
This is the most cited figure in the estimator for the lifecycle savings case. The remaining 22% — surface wear, drainage, signage — is retained as ongoing maintenance in both scenarios.

Certified CBR test result on field-treated soil. This is the basis for the In-Place BaseGrade CBR default and the a2 = 0.20 layer coefficient assignment. The 125% result exceeds the standard crushed stone threshold (100%) — the slider maximum.

In-Place BaseGrade product cost
Cost per square yard of treated surface for the In-Place BaseGrade liquid product and application. Depends on three site-specific variables: dilution ratio (typically 4:1 to 5:1, determined by proctor test), application rate (gallons of mixed solution per cubic yard), and treatment depth. These interact to produce a project-specific cost.

Proxy representing a partial overlay or surface rehabilitation event. No published national figure exists for this ratio. 60% is the constructed midpoint between a minor chip seal (20–40%) and a full reconstruction (300–500%).

How to read this in a procurement or engineering context

The estimator is strongest where it draws from AASHTO engineering constants and FHWA cost data — the structural number calculations, base depth recommendations, and maintenance cost baseline are all grounded in federal standards that engineers recognize and accept. These figures can be presented in a procurement context with full source citations.

The estimator is more exposed where it relies on In-Place BaseGrade field documentation for behavioral assumptions — lifecycle extension and project duration are based on a limited number of documented projects. These figures should be presented as planning-level projections, not engineering specifications, until additional field documentation accumulates.

The four pending confirmation items — product cost per SY, treatment pass labor, base placement rate verification, and conventional project duration — should be resolved before using this estimator in any formal procurement, board presentation, or bid document. All four are user-editable fields. The estimator flags them prominently in the calculator interface.
